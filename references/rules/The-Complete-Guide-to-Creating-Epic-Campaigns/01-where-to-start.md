# Where to Start?

*Page 8*

> Someone wants something badly by a specific time and is having difficulty getting it using something because of reasons.

Fans of the channel, "How to be a Great GM", will know that this is my turn-to phrase – well, at least perhaps the simpler version: Someone wants something badly and is having difficulty getting it. It is the only plot that we as a species on planet Earth have managed to invent that satisfies us from a narrative perspective. Any story that isn't considered 'art' will use this as its fundamental backbone. This means that we too need to use it in our games. And if you do, your storytelling, your plots and your adventures will be second to none.

## IMPORTANT
*The Master Plot is very different from the story.*

A Master Plot is the general idea of what is happening in the background. It is the events that are occurring whether the players' characters are there or not. It is the overarching narrative that drives the campaign. The story is what the players' characters experience, the adventures they go on, the people they meet, the choices they make. The Master Plot is the lake; the story is the ripples on the surface.

Think of it this way: if you were to describe your campaign to someone who had never played in it, the Master Plot would be what you'd tell them. "It's about an evil empress trying to return from the dead and the heroes who must stop her." That's the Master Plot. The story would be all the individual adventures, the characters they met, the choices they made, the failures and successes along the way.

## The Lake Metaphor

I want you to think of your Master Plot as a lake. A deep, still body of water. Now, when your players' characters do something – anything – it creates ripples on the surface of that lake. Those ripples are the story. But the lake itself, the Master Plot, remains largely unchanged. It's still there, still deep, still the foundation for everything that happens on the surface.

The beauty of this metaphor is that it helps you understand that your Master Plot should be robust enough to handle whatever your players throw at it. If they kill the wrong person, or go in the wrong direction, or make the wrong choice, the lake is still there. You just need to figure out how their actions create new ripples.

## Building Your Master Plot

So, let's build a Master Plot. I'm going to use an example that we'll develop throughout this book. Let's say we want to create a campaign about an evil empress who is trying to return from the dead. Here's how we might develop that using our formula:

**Someone wants something badly by a specific time and is having difficulty getting it using something because of reasons.**

Let's break this down:

### Someone

This, in most cases is your primary Nemesis. Now hang on, I know, roleplaying is supposed to be about the characters' journey and the hero's decisions. Yes, I agree. But for there to be heroes, there needs to be something for them to be heroes about. Luke Skywalker wouldn't be awesome if the Empire wasn't being evil, Sherlock Holmes wouldn't be a great detective if no crime was committed. Your players' characters simply cannot be heroes if there is nothing to hero against. So, who is that person they must hero against? Your Nemesis (I'll unpack Nemesis, villain, and henchman later).

So back to this Someone...

Who is this someone? In the next chapter we handle how to make this someone awesome. For now, let's call her Empress Ilianna. She's dead, but she's not gone. She's trapped in some kind of limbo, and she wants to return to the land of the living to reclaim her throne.

### Wants Something

What does Empress Ilianna want? She wants to be resurrected. But that's not specific enough. She wants to be resurrected in a way that will ensure her power is absolute and unquestionable. She wants to return to life with enough power to crush any who oppose her.

### Badly

How badly does she want this? She wants it so badly that she's willing to sacrifice anything and anyone to achieve it. She's been planning this for decades, maybe centuries. She's been manipulating events from beyond the grave, setting up the pieces for her return.

### By a Specific Time

When does she need this to happen? Let's say she needs to be resurrected by the spring equinox, when the veil between the worlds is thinnest. That gives us a deadline, a ticking clock that will drive the tension of our campaign.

### Having Difficulty Getting It

What's stopping her? Well, she's dead for one thing. But more than that, she needs specific components for her resurrection ritual. She needs loyal servants to carry out her plans. She needs a suitable body to inhabit. She needs powerful magical artifacts to fuel the ritual.

### Using Something

What is she using to try to achieve her goal? She's using a network of cultists and dark nobles – let's call them the Dark Dukes – who are still loyal to her. She's using her knowledge of dark magic and forbidden rituals. She's using her ability to communicate from beyond the grave.

### Because of Reasons

What's making it difficult for her? There's a rebellion against her rule, even though she's dead. There are heroes who oppose her. The components she needs are scattered and well-guarded. The ritual itself is dangerous and could fail catastrophically.

So, putting it all together:

**Empress Ilianna wants to ensure the loyalty of the dark dukes who make up her realm by the day of her resurrection but, she is having difficulty securing that loyalty using the threat of destruction because of the rebellion.**

### When?

This answer is easy – before her resurrection. The entire Master Plot hinges on that moment in time. We could choose that to be in 4 years, 4 months, or if we really wanted an intense game within 4 days. But for now, let us work it as within 4 months. Spring will burst into being and Her Highness will be resurrected. I'm under a lot of stress to write this book. Spring.

### Why?

Why before her resurrection? Well, why is she being resurrected in the first place? Time for one of those moments of frantic ceiling watching... What if she was being resurrected because she is actually dead, ruling from an exiled plane she was cast into, or something? Her resurrection is because the dark dukes have found the perfect host into which to place her essence, and they are the only ones who can perform the ritual. But they need to be loyal to her, or they might just decide to use the ritual for their own purposes.

### How?

How is she going to ensure their loyalty? By demonstrating her power, even from beyond the grave. By rewarding those who serve her and punishing those who betray her. By making it clear that her return is inevitable and that those who oppose her will suffer.

## The Lord of the Rings Example

Let's look at how this works in a story we all know: The Lord of the Rings.

**Someone wants something badly by a specific time and is having difficulty getting it using something because of reasons.**

**Sauron wants to reclaim the One Ring before the Free Peoples can mount an effective resistance and is having difficulty getting it using his armies and servants because Frodo has it and is trying to destroy it.**

See how that works? Sauron is the Someone. He wants the Ring (something). He wants it badly – it's the source of his power. He needs it by a specific time – before his enemies can destroy it or unite against him. He's having difficulty getting it because Frodo has it and is actively trying to destroy it. He's using his armies, the Nazgûl, Saruman, and other servants to try to get it.

The entire Lord of the Rings is built around this simple sentence. Everything that happens in the story is either Sauron trying to get the Ring, or the heroes trying to stop him.

## Making It Work

Now, here's the key: your Master Plot sentence needs to be functional before it's amazing. Don't worry about making it brilliant or original or earth-shattering. Worry about making it work. A functional plot that you can actually run is infinitely better than a brilliant plot that falls apart the moment your players do something unexpected.

Once you have a functional Master Plot, then you can start adding the details, the twists, the surprises, the amazing moments that will make your campaign memorable. But start with functional.

In the next chapter, we'll talk about how to create a Nemesis that your players will love to hate, and how to tie them into your Master Plot in a way that creates compelling, ongoing conflict.
