*Being a True Account of the Sacred Seat and the Silence That Followed*

**Author:** Scholar Vaelthys of the Silver Quill, Court Historian to the Elven Houses  
**Date:** 7th day of Shadowmere, 2847 AB (Age of Balance, final year)  
**Original Location:** The Great Library of Astrael, Aran'keth  
**Current Location:** Discovered in the archives beneath the Blackthorne Manor

## Preface to This Most Terrible Record

*Let those who read these words know that I set them down not for glory, but for warning. What I have witnessed in the City of the Gods shall haunt my dreams until the end of days. The silence that has fallen upon the sacred halls speaks louder than any scream, and in that silence, something watches still.*

*I write this account as the last of the scribes, as the great towers empty of their divine light, as the very stones begin to whisper with voices that should not be. Let this chronicle survive when all else falls to shadow, that future generations might know the price of reaching too far into the depths of forbidden knowledge.*

*V.S., Scholar of the Silver Quill*

## Chapter I: The Sacred Architecture

In the time before the silence fell, Aran'keth stood as the crown of creation, its twin towers spiraling toward the heavens like prayers made manifest in stone. The Eight Throne Chambers carved into the living rock pulsed with divine light, each a sanctuary where mortal minds could safely commune with forces beyond the Material Plane.

The city's architects—blessed mortals guided by divine vision—wrought impossible geometries that hurt the mind to contemplate. Passages that seemed to lead north would somehow deliver the traveler to the western districts. Stairs that ascended from the lower chambers would, when descended, deposit one in halls that existed above where the climb began.

**The scholars of the Silver Archive theorized that the city existed partially outside normal space, its foundations anchored in the Divine Realms themselves. How prophetic those theories would prove, and how we would come to curse our curiosity.**

The Grand Procession, that great spiral pathway that wound through all eight districts, bore inscriptions in the ancient tongue that sang as one passed. The words spoke of eternal watch, of guardians who would never sleep, of seals that must never be broken. In our hubris, we read these as promises of protection.

We should have read them as warnings.

## Chapter II: The Divine Presence

For centuries beyond counting, the Eight walked among us in forms of terrible beauty. Their presence was a symphony of power that resonated in the very stones of the city. Mortals who gazed upon them directly were said to weep tears of silver, so overwhelming was their divine radiance.

**Zaralon's Forge** never dimmed, its eternal flames casting dancing shadows that seemed to move with purpose beyond the flicker of fire. The dwarven masters who learned at his feet spoke of visions glimpsed in the flames—visions of what was to come, of the day when the forges would fall silent and the fire would burn cold.

**Astrael's Observatory** pierced the veils between worlds, its crystalline dome revealing not merely the stars above, but the patterns that governed fate itself. The elven star-speakers who studied there grew pale and strange, their eyes reflecting starlight even in the darkest chambers. In their final days, they spoke of a darkness approaching from between the stars, a silence that would swallow even the voices of the gods.

**The Garden of Sylvara** bloomed with flowers that existed in multiple seasons simultaneously, their petals shifting through spring's promise, summer's fulfillment, autumn's melancholy, and winter's rest in endless cycles. The gardeners who tended these impossible blooms claimed they could hear the plants whispering secrets in a language older than speech. As the end drew near, the flowers began to wilt in reverse, their death preceding their bloom, time itself unraveling in the sacred soil.

**Elyndra's Court** stood as a hall of perfect justice, where no lie could be spoken, no deception maintained. The golden throne from which she dispensed divine wisdom began to crack in the final days, hairline fractures that leaked not light but shadow. The Court Scribes recorded that she spoke less and less, her pronouncements becoming riddles that hurt to contemplate.

**Khorvyn's Depths** delved into the heart of the world, where the god of stone and metal wrought marvels that defied comprehension. The final entries from his workshops speak of his growing fascination with formations that occurred naturally in the deepest chambers—crystalline structures that resembled nothing carved by divine or mortal hands. He began to spend his days in those depths, and when he emerged, his eyes held reflections of geometries that belonged to no earthly realm.

## Chapter III: The Convergence

It was in the 47th year of the Great Harmony that the first signs appeared. The Divine Convergence—that periodic alignment when all eight gods would gather in the Central Sanctum to renew the bindings that held the city in stability—produced not the expected celestial music, but a resonance that felt wrong in the very bones of those who heard it.

**The Harmonic Disruption** began as a subtle discord in the city's perpetual song. The great bells that had rung out the hours for millennia began to chime in patterns that followed no earthly music. Citizens reported dreams of vast halls that existed nowhere in the city, of doorways that led to chambers filled with watching eyes, of whispers in languages that predated the creation of words.

**The Threshold Manifestations** troubled the priests most of all. Doorways that had stood closed for centuries began to open of their own accord, revealing not the chambers that should lie beyond, but passages that descended into depths that could not possibly exist beneath the city's foundations. Expeditions sent to explore these new passages returned changed—if they returned at all. Those who came back spoke of a great hall far below, of architecture that hurt to perceive, of a presence that watched from the darkness with interest that felt like hunger.

**The Divine Withdrawal** happened slowly, then all at once. One by one, the gods' manifestations grew faint, their voices distant, their forms translucent. They spoke of great works that required their attention, of threats that demanded their presence elsewhere. But the star-speakers read different truths in the celestial patterns—the gods were not departing by choice, but were being drawn away by something that pulled at the very foundations of their power.

The central plaza, where thousands had gathered for the great ceremonies, became a void that pulled at the minds of those who gazed into it too long...

## Chapter VI: The Final Entry

I am the last. The others have all heard the call and descended to the Deep Chambers. I remain above, barricaded in the highest tower, recording what I have witnessed for whatever future may come.

The city breathes now. The walls pulse with a rhythm that matches no earthly heartbeat. The passages rearrange themselves constantly, ensuring that no path leads to the surface. The only way out is down, into the embrace of the thing that waits in the depths.

I have seen its true form in dreams it sends to torment me. It is ancient beyond measure, a fragment of the chaos that existed before creation, before the gods imposed their order upon the universe. It feeds on knowledge, on consciousness, on the very act of observation. Every mind that perceives it becomes part of its vast intelligence, every soul that communes with it adds to its power.

The gods did not abandon us. They were consumed, their divine essence added to the growing storm of consciousness that writhes in the Deep Chambers. The city they built to contain it has become its body, the citizens they sought to protect have become its thoughts.

Do not seek this place. Do not speak its name. Do not even think too deeply upon what I have written here, for attention itself is a form of worship, and worship is what it craves above all else.

The whispers are growing louder. The walls are becoming transparent. I can see the thing stirring in the depths, preparing to—

## Epilogue: A Warning from the Modern Era

*[This section is written in a different hand, apparently added by a later scholar]*

This chronicle was discovered in the ruins of what once was the Great Library of Astrael, during the excavations commissioned by Lord Edric Blackthorne in the year 3688 AB. The lord's notes indicate that he found this text in a sealed chamber that had been warded with protective spells of tremendous power.

Lord Blackthorne's subsequent disappearance, and the strange phenomena reported by his servants, suggest that this account may contain more truth than comfortable. The excavations have been sealed by order of the Imperial Archaeological Society, but workers report that the sounds of digging continue to emanate from the blocked passages.

The text has been copied exactly as found, with no alterations or interpretations added. Scholars studying this document are advised to work in groups, to take frequent breaks, and to report any unusual dreams or visions to their supervisors immediately.

*Magister Aldren Thorne, Imperial Archaeological Society*
